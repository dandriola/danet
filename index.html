Hello and thanks for visiting my page! I'd love to tell you a bit about myself. I'm originally  from northeastern Pennsylvania and have been intrigued by languages and cultures from a young age. I completed my BA in Deaf Studies at California State University in Northridge in 2013 where I became fascinated by the study of the brain and learning. In 2014, I entered the PhD in Educational Neuroscience (PEN) program at Gallaudet and was advised by Dr. Clifton Langdon in his Language and Educational Neuroscience (LENS) Laboratory.  

My research focuses on the abstract linguistic mechanisms that support reading development, namely phonological awareness. Using functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) neuroimaging, I study early reading skills in deaf and hearing children and am very active in science communication and outreach. One of my favorite projects has been serving as the lead coordinator of Neuroscience Day for Gallaudet University's Bison Brainiacs Program, which gives Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing high school students hands-on learning in the sciences in American Sign Language from Deaf scientists and doctoral students. I've also worked with teams of students to translate science findings into American Sign Language.


As a scientist dedicated to improving science communication, I attended the American Association for the Advancement of Science (AAAS) Catalyzing Advocacy in Science and Engineering (CASE) Workshop and the flagship ComSciCon annual workshop.

My Research

Publications and Presentations

How do children learn to read?  That's the question that lead me to pursuing my Ph.D. at Gallaudet University. I study how deaf children who use sign language develop literacy skills with limited or no access to sound.

This research has lead to co-authoring a paper outlining a new theoretical model of reading as well as numerous national and international presentations.

Science Outreach

As a first generation student, it is one of my goals to help younger generations to see themselves as leaders and to connect with science in real-world, hands-on ways. Since 2016, I've been coordinating the Neuroscience Day programming for Gallaudet University's Bison Brainiacs Program. 

Bison Brainiacs brings Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing high school students to Gallaudet University for a week of science immersion. Students learn marine biology, robotics, and neuroscience for Deaf, Hard-of-Hearing, and hearing professors and graduate students. 

Connecting with science in a tangible way, with teachers who students can see themselves in, encourages students from diverse backgrounds and abilities to pursue their curiosities and explore opportunities in STEM.

Science Translation

With a team of students in 2014, I helped organize a two-day research translation workshop to translate scientific research into American Sign Language videos. Our goal is to make research that concerns the deaf community, educators, and parents of deaf children accessible to those stakeholders. This type of work is critical in the translation between science and society. In total 10 research briefs were translated into ASL and have received more than 10,000 views on YouTube. 

Science Consulting

To better serve the more than 5,000 schools around the globe serving children ages 3 - 19, the International Baccalaureate Organization conducted an international Bilingual and Multilingual Teaching and Learning Workshop for its thousands of educators and administrators. I consulted on the workshop materials, providing additional resources and evidence-based research and strategies for teaching bilingual and multilingual learners.
